MAXENT ARCHAEOLOGICAL PREDICTION MODEL IN NORTHWEST HONG KONG

1. Introduction: Predictive Modelling in Archaeology

This study attempts to generate a prediction map of prehistoric archaeological sites in northwest Hong Kong using the MaxEnt algorithm.

The use of GIS and related spatial technologies have played a significant role in archaeology, especially to understand human and their relationship with the natural environment (Conolly 2008). One of earliest and widespread uses of GIS in archaeology is the development of predictive model.

Predictive modelling have been used in archaeology to develop predictive models for archaeological site presence. This is done based on the premise that a certain type of archaeological site tends to occur at the same kind of “place” (Renfrew and Bahn 2016), where “place” can be quantified into different kind of environmental spatial data, including elevation, aspect and gradient, etc. Maximum Entropy Modelling (MaxEnt) is a machine learning model that compares environmental variables and the location of presence archaeological sites data only to calculate the probability of archaeological presence. This approach avoids the need for absence data and provides quantitative results for interpretation by archaeologists.

MaxEnt model has been widely used in ecology and species distribution modelling, but it has also been successfully applied in archaeology due to its ability to use presence data only. The sampling of presence data avoids the need to create a set of site absence data, which is especially useful in cases where the absence of data does not necessarily mean the absence of archaeological presence (Kvamme 2006; Yaworsky et al. 2020). The MaxEnt algorithm calculates the probability of archaeological presence based on the comparison of environmental variables and the location of presence data. The model generates a prediction map that shows the likelihood of archaeological presence in different locations, which can then be interpreted by archaeologists with quantitative results.

Predictive modelling using MaxEnt has become increasingly popular in archaeology due to its accuracy and efficiency. It allows archaeologists to study a widespread extent where not every location can be studied comprehensively. The resulting prediction map provides a useful tool for identifying potential archaeological sites, which can then be investigated further.

2.3 Model Input and Output

The MaxEnt model allows for the optimization of its effectiveness and accuracy through different parameter settings. To enhance accuracy, multiple test runs were conducted using various parameters, environmental variables, presence data, and processing extents. In this study, the model was first configured using the software's default parameters to establish a baseline. Then, through iterative test runs, the parameters and input dataset were optimized to maximize the model's performance.

The model generates output consisting of a continuous prediction map in standardised ASCII The model also produces:

  • a table presenting the ‘percent contribution’ and ‘permutation importance’, indicating the degree to which each variable contributes to the model and the significance of each variable.
  • response curves of each environmental variable, displaying the predicted probability against the range of values for each environmental variable. These provide insight into the variables ranges associated with higher predicted probability of containing archaeological sites.
  • an Area Under Curve (AUC) output, which is utilised as a metric for evaluating the effectiveness of the model at predicting archaeological potential.

2.4 Model Evaluation

One way to assess the performance of MaxEnt is by calculating the Area Under Curve (AUC) of the Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curve. This method involves determining how well the model can differentiate positive locations (where archaeological potential areas are present) from negative locations (where no archaeological potential exists). AUC values range from 0 to 1, with higher values indicating better model performance. An AUC value of 0.5 suggests that there is no correlation between the environmental variables and the archaeological potential predictions by MaxEnt. In this study, the AUC values of the MaxEnt prediction model were calculated by the MaxEnt software.

Furthermore, cross-validation was conducted by running the model while dividing the presence datasets into training dataset and testing dataset. The model will be trained using the training dataset and tested against the testing dataset to evaluate the model. This will be replicated multiple times with different splits of the presence datasets and provide an average calculation of the model performance. The process is enabled as a parameters in the MaxEnt model software.

To ensure satisfactory predictiveness, repeated test runs are done and reviewed with different parameters and modified datasets if neccesary. The model is evaluated with each run until the the model is satisfactory with high AUC value while avoiding overfitting.

3 Results

3.1 Introduction

This section displays the results generated by the historical and prehistoric prediction modelas by MaxEnt. Three crucial results for each environmental variable are generated, namely percent contribution, permutation importance and response curves of each environmental variables. Other results are the Jackknife analysis.

Percent contribution and permutation importance are two metrics the help to evaluate the relative importance of the environmental variables for the prediction of archaeological potential by MaxEnt model. Percent contribution measures the weight of particular variable contributed to the model results based on how MaxEnt algorithm utilise it. The calculation was made by the MaxEnt algorithm by first making predictions based on only one variable at a time. Then another variable is added to the algorithm to check if the predictions has improved and assigns a contribution score based on that improvement. The variables that improve the predictions the most get higher contribution scores.

On the other hand, permutation importance measures the relevancy of each variable in predicting the selection of past human settlement location. It works by randomly mixing up the values of one variable at a time, then seeing how much the prediction model of archaeological potential changes as a result. This change would trigger decrease to the AUC-ROC value. The more this change cause decrease in the AUC-ROC value, the more important is this variable to the prediction model.

Furthermore, response curves portray how the predicted archaeological potential varies across the range of values for respective environmental variable. The y-axis of the graph is the predicted probability, and the x-axis is the value of variables. Curves shapes reveals conditions of each variable that favours higher or lower potential per statistical patterns. It should be noted that these curves only consider single variable in isolation and overlooks the correlations between variables.

3.3 Response Curves of Variables

The response curves illustrate how environmental variables influence the MaxEnt model results. It reveals the variables that has the most impact on predicting archaeological potential. Curves that change sharply and differ across the entire range of a variable likely indicate that the variable significantly affected where ancient human sites were formed.

On the other hand, curves that remain fairly similar and give an even probability across all values for a variable tend to represent aspects with minimal relevance for determining which landscape features were more favourable for ancient human settlement and activities. These variables typically contribute very little to the prediction, correctly reflecting their weak predictive power considered individually.

In simple terms, the steeper and more variable the curve for an environmental factor, the more important that factor likely was in influencing where ancient humans chose to settle and the activities they undertook.  However, factors with flatter, more uniform curves probably did not significantly impact ancient human site locations and behaviours.  The response curves of environmental variables for the MaxEnt model in prehistoric period is displayed in Plate 3.3.1.

3.4 Summary

Response curve analysis suggests two general environmental settings exhibited relatively higher archaeological potential during the prehistoric period:

  1. Coastal flats:
    • Lower elevations (0m to 50m)
    • Gentler slopes (0° to 10°)
    • Proximity to the coast (0m to 500m and 3000m to 3500m)
    • Proximity to alluvial deposits (0m to 250m from Qa)
  1. Hill foots:
    • Foot slopes and gentle slopes (<15°)
    • Aspect facing Deep Bay in the northwest
    • Proximity to the hill (within 400m)

Overall, the coastal flats appeared to offer the most favourable conditions for past human settlement and activity. Lower-lying alluvial areas near the coast but set back from active beach zones may have balanced advantages of resources, arable land and transport routes with fewer constraints.

Hill foots likely conferred advantages due to prospects, aspect, drainage and river views, though they exhibited less potential than coastal flats. Top of hills showed the lowest potential, indicating humans preferred slopes over peaks during the prehistoric period. Furthermore, direction to hills exhibited no significant influence, suggesting relative orientation to hills mattered little compared to other factors like slope, elevation and coastality.

In summary, landscape setting appears to have strongly influenced archaeological potential during the prehistoric period, with coastal flats offering the most favourable conditions according to statistical patterns in the input data.

Presence data for locations with known archaeological potential during the prehistoric period exhibit two general patterns:

  1. Coastal flats: Sites occurring on the coastal plains tend to cluster in Tuen Mun, including at Lau Fau Shan, Ngau Hom Shek and Mong Tseng. The environmental variable values for these locations - like low elevation, proximity to alluvial deposits and the coast - generally fall within the high probability ranges indicated by the response curves.
  1. Hill foots: Sites situated on gently sloping hills tend to concentrate in Fanling and Sheung Shui, particularly around Wah Shan and Hung Leng. The environmental characteristics of these locations - such as slope, aspect and distance to hills - also typically align with the higher probability ranges revealed in the corresponding curves.

This correspondence between the environmental conditions at known archaeological locations and the high probability ranges predicted by the model strengthens confidence in the response curves' accuracy. They appear to have successfully identified the key landscape variables influencing site distributions and potential during the prehistoric period. 4 Archaeological Potential of Prehistoric Period

4.1 Introduction

The predictive model can provide useful initial indication of archaeological potential, but it should be used cautiously and cannot be taken at face value due to inherent limitations. While the environmentally deterministic approach has been criticised for minimising cultural factors, it also has value in identifying potential environmental influences on past human decisions. Biases in the data and methodology can affect the accuracy of the results. Hence, archaeological investigation under this Project and previous archaeological studies are essential to complement, validate and refine the output of the predictive modelling, establishing archaeological potential areas with high confidence.

Previous archaeological findings from prehistoric period have uncovered evidence of past human occupation in Hong Kong.  Archaeological sites from the prehistory have been discovered on the relatively flat area along coastal beaches in the western Hong Kong near the Zhujiang River Delta. Prehistoric archaeological remains have been found in locations such as Lau Fau Shan, Ngau Hom Shek and Mong Tseng.  The geographical condition in these areas would have made them attractive places for human settlement during the prehistoric period.

The area has a coastal landscape that would have favoured prehistoric human settlements (see Figure 1). Hop Shing Wai Area

Hop Shing Wai Area is similar to Mai Po Area in having a coastal landscape that would have supported prehistoric human settlements (see Figure 1). The land sits at an elevation approximately between +4mPD and +43mPD (mean = +9mPD) with slopes generally lower than 10°. The area is close to the coast, between 30m and 295m approximately. Such coastal setting has known historical settlement such as the village cluster at San Tin, and the Mai Po Lo Wai, as well as known prehistoric coastal setting in the southwestern coast of Deep Bay in Tuen Mun. In the light of this, this piece of land is predicted to contain archaeological potential in prehistoric period.

San Tin Area

The area has known historical potential as the villages in San Tin could be dated to the early Ming dynasty (see Figure 1). The San Tin Area is a flat coastal plan with an elevation between +2mPD and +10mPD and slopes of 0° to 4° The historical villages of San Tin is situated in front of a small hill facing northwest.
